DIY & Construction June 30, 2017

3D Printed USV Hull Files Available on Hackaday

Building the n3m0 autonomous boat: A comprehensive guide to 3D printing your own USV hull

Kevin K

USV Enthusiast & Builder

The world of DIY unmanned surface vehicles has taken an exciting turn with the release of 3D printable hull files for the n3m0 autonomous boat project. This innovative approach to USV construction opens up new possibilities for hobbyists, researchers, and marine technology enthusiasts to build their own autonomous vessels using readily available 3D printing technology.

The n3m0 Project Overview

The n3m0 autonomous boat project, originally featured on Hackaday, represents a significant milestone in democratizing USV technology. The project leader generously made the 3D files available for download, allowing builders worldwide to create their own autonomous vessels. The design features a sleek, functional hull with integrated compartments for electronics and propulsion systems.

Project Specifications

  • • Approximately 1 meter in length when complete
  • • Designed for Pixhawk 2.1 flight controller
  • • Accommodates 2x BlueRobotics thrusters
  • • Integrated top hatch for electronics sealing
  • • Eliminates need for external Pelican cases

3D Printing Challenges and Solutions

Building a watertight hull through 3D printing presents unique challenges that require careful consideration and problem-solving. Kevin K, who documented his build process, encountered several issues that are valuable lessons for future builders.

The primary challenge involved layer delamination during the printing process, likely caused by cooler room temperatures and the absence of an enclosure for the 3D printer. This resulted in gaps between layers that required extensive filling and sanding to achieve a smooth, watertight surface.

Waterproofing Solutions

Achieving waterproof integrity proved to be one of the most challenging aspects of the project. After struggling with persistent leaks that were difficult to locate, Kevin eventually covered the entire hull in yellow spray-on PlastiDip, which successfully created a waterproof barrier.

Mike Holden, the designer of n3m0, shared his approach to waterproofing: using two coats of epoxy laminating resin to seal the hull. While this added some weight to the vessel, it provided reliable waterproofing. He also noted that designing the hull skins to be 4mm thick and printing them with hollow infill, while making them light and stiff, made waterproofing more challenging.

Lessons Learned and Future Improvements

The project provided valuable insights into the practical challenges of 3D printing large-scale marine vessels. Kevin eventually discontinued the project due to persistent sealing issues, but his experience offers important lessons for future builders.

Material Considerations

ABS filament is recommended for better durability and waterproofing properties compared to PLA, though it requires more precise printing conditions.

Design Optimization

Reducing hull thickness to 2mm and printing solid sections can improve waterproofing while maintaining structural integrity.

Thruster Integration and Performance

The n3m0 design accommodates dual BlueRobotics thrusters, providing excellent maneuverability and power for its size. Mike Holden, who uses similar thrusters on his larger boat "Dumbo," notes that while the power is sufficient, prop pitch optimization may be necessary for optimal speed performance.

One consideration mentioned is the potential for debris to become lodged in the thruster fans, particularly in areas with high levels of floating trash or during periods of increased runoff. This is an important factor to consider when planning operational environments for 3D printed USVs.

Cost and Accessibility Benefits

One of the most significant advantages of the 3D printed approach is the cost-effectiveness and accessibility it provides. Mike Holden reported using less than 2 rolls of PLA filament for his complete hull, making this an extremely affordable option for USV construction compared to traditional boat building or purchasing commercial hulls.

The open-source nature of the project, combined with the availability of 3D printing services worldwide, means that builders can access this technology regardless of their location or fabrication capabilities. This democratization of USV technology has the potential to accelerate innovation in autonomous marine systems.

Conclusion and Future Outlook

The n3m0 project represents an important step forward in making USV technology accessible to hobbyists, researchers, and small organizations. While challenges remain in achieving reliable waterproofing with 3D printed hulls, the lessons learned from this project provide a foundation for future improvements.

As 3D printing technology continues to advance, with improvements in materials, precision, and waterproofing techniques, we can expect to see more successful implementations of printed marine vessels. The combination of open-source design, community support, and iterative improvement makes this an exciting area for continued development in the USV community.

For those interested in building their own 3D printed USV, the n3m0 project files remain available on Hackaday, along with the valuable community insights and lessons learned from early adopters. This project serves as both an inspiration and a practical starting point for the next generation of DIY unmanned surface vehicles.
